Cross-category equivocation

May 28 2012

In James Conant’s essay on types of philosophical nonsense in Wittgenstein’s Tractatus and Rudolf Carnap’s writing, he lists several newspaper headlines as examples of “cross-category equivocation,” or examples of sentences whose words have multiple meanings and aren’t clear about which meaning they mean. Forget the philosophical relevance of these, I just think they’re funny to read:

  • British Left Waffles on Falkland Islands
  • British Push Bottles Up German Rear
  • Potential Witness to Murder Drunk
  • Legal Aid Advocates Worry
  • Crowds Rushing To See Pope Trample 6 to Death
  • Beating Witness Provides Names
  • Nixon Stands Pat on Watergate Tapes
  • University Studies Mushroom
  • Carter Plans Swell Deficit

It’s good to see that philosophers are keeping track of things like this.
